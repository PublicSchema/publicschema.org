# ADR-009: Consent and data governance, mirroring W3C DPV and ISO/IEC TS 27560

**Status:** Draft

## Context

PublicSchema models the people, groups, events, and artefacts involved in public program delivery. It does not yet model the **data-governance metadata** that accompanies those records: the consent given (or not) by data subjects, the legal basis for processing, the privacy notice shown at collection, the purposes for which data may be used, the categories of personal data involved, the recipients with whom it may be shared, and the evidence that these things actually happened.

Every adopter needs this layer. Data protection laws (GDPR in Europe, Kenya DPA 2019, POPIA in South Africa, LGPD in Brazil, DPDP 2023 in India, DPA 2012 in the Philippines) converge on roughly the same record shape: who consented or did not, to what processing, for what purpose, under what legal basis, shown what notice, with what evidence, valid from when until when. Programs that share beneficiary data with partners decide per record, per recipient, per purpose whether sharing is authorised. Systems that issue Verifiable Credentials record the lawful basis that backed the issuance. Humanitarian and emergency programs document processing that is not based on consent at all.

Three facts make this a PublicSchema concern rather than a deferral to adopters:

1. **Standards exist and converge.** W3C Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV v2, 2024), ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 (Consent record information structure), ISO/IEC 29184:2020 (Online privacy notices and consent), and Kantara Consent Receipt v1.1 share the same conceptual model: a notice defines scope, a record captures agreement or documents lawful processing, the record names parties and processing terms and validity. DPV v2 is the richest machine-readable expression of the shared model and was co-developed with ISO/IEC 27560 contributors.
2. **Convergence across adopters is real.** OpenSPP has a production-grade DPV-aligned module (`spp_consent`) implementing ISO 27560 with JSON-LD export. OpenIMIS has a narrower consent surface. Program MIS systems in Kenya, the Philippines, Indonesia, Brazil, Colombia, Uganda, and Lebanon each re-invent a partial version of the same schema. A common vocabulary lets them interoperate without dictating implementation.
3. **Operational reality constrains the shape.** Field practice (paper-first intake, multilingual notice presentation, witnessed or thumbprint consent, bulk-intake community sessions, withdrawal through community workers) does not fit the click-to-accept model assumed by many standards implementations. The schema has to accommodate paper-to-digitisation gaps, language of notice presentation, witnesses, and withdrawal channels that include non-digital pathways.

**Status of W3C DPV v2.** DPV is a W3C Community Group report, not a W3C Recommendation. It is actively maintained by the DPVCG and serves as the de-facto machine-readable consent vocabulary. Adjacent W3C Recommendations (Verifiable Credentials Data Model 2.0, PROV-O, ODRL, DID Core) cover related but distinct ground. This ADR mirrors DPV structurally and cites the Recommendations where they apply. DPV's CG status is a versioning risk called out in Consequences.

**Scope boundary.** This ADR adds the record structure and the vocabulary layer. It does not add full consent lifecycle enforcement (state transitions as validators, notice-as-boundary validation, runtime consent-check APIs, audit trails), data subject rights requests (access, erasure, portability, rectification, objection), retention policies as first-class concepts, data-sharing agreements between organisations, records of processing activities (GDPR Art 30), or data breach records. Those are governance concepts adjacent to consent and are deferred to named follow-on ADRs.

## Decision

Decisions are grouped:

- A. Concept inventory (1 to 5)
- B. `ConsentRecord` shape (6 to 13)
- C. `PrivacyNotice` shape (14)
- D. Operational properties required by field practice (15 to 19)
- E. Interoperability hygiene (20 to 22)
- F. Non-decisions documented here (23 to 25)

### A. Concept inventory

1. **Add `ConsentRecord` as a concrete concept at the root namespace** (`publicschema.org/ConsentRecord`). It records the lawful processing of a data subject's personal data, aligned with ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023's four-section structure (header, parties, processing, consent specifics) and `dpv:ConsentRecord`. The name matches DPV and ISO 27560 convention. The concept's first sentence makes this explicit: records with `legal_basis ≠ consent` still use the name; this is the published convention, not an endorsement of labelling non-consent processing as consent. Maturity: `draft`.
2. **Add `PrivacyNotice` as a concrete concept at the root namespace** (`publicschema.org/PrivacyNotice`). It represents the notice shown to data subjects before collection or processing, aligned with ISO/IEC 29184:2020 and `dpv:PrivacyNotice`. Where processing is consent-based, the same concept also serves the role of `dpv:ConsentNotice`. Maturity: `draft`.
3. **Reference W3C DPV URIs directly for purposes, personal data categories, and processing operations. Do not define parallel PublicSchema concepts or vocabularies for these axes.** The three DPV class trees (`dpv:Purpose`, `dpv-pd:PersonalData`, `dpv:Processing`) are rich, actively curated by the DPVCG, and domain-neutral. `ConsentRecord.purposes`, `ConsentRecord.personal_data_categories`, `ConsentRecord.processing_operations`, `PrivacyNotice.purposes`, and `PrivacyNotice.data_categories` range at URI. Values are DPV URIs (preferred) or adopter-defined URIs that are subclasses of the corresponding DPV class. PublicSchema publishes a curated starter list of DPV URIs relevant to public program delivery in `docs/dpv-vocabulary-reference.md` as guidance, not as a schema artefact. Precedent: ISO 3166 countries are referenced, not forked; DPV class trees are referenced, not forked. Forking DPV's hierarchies into PublicSchema concepts would duplicate W3C work, introduce drift, and offer no operational value because these values are referenced on records, not themselves records with lifecycles.
4. **Mirror W3C DPV v2 structurally** where DPV covers our ground. Concept names match DPV class names. Property names use PublicSchema's snake_case convention but map one-to-one to DPV `has*` properties in the JSON-LD context (`data_subject` ↔ `dpv:hasDataSubject`, `controllers` ↔ `dpv:hasDataController`, etc.). Extensions beyond DPV (paper-workflow fields, language fields, witness fields) are marked as PublicSchema extensions in both YAML and JSON-LD export, using a `ps:` namespace that does not claim DPV compliance for those fields. Mirroring (rather than mapping via `system_mappings` alone) reduces divergence risk, resolves concept-versus-vocabulary debates in favour of DPV's hierarchy, and gives adopters DPV compliance by default. Versioning risk (DPV v3 may change URIs) is addressed in decision 20.
5. **Keep the following as closed vocabularies** (non-hierarchical enumerations, each value carrying a DPV URI where applicable):
   - `legal-basis`: six GDPR Art 6 codes mapped to `dpv-gdpr:A6-1-a` through `A6-1-f`. Maturity `candidate` (values are law-locked).
   - `special-category-basis`: values aligned with GDPR Art 9(2)(a) to (j) and equivalent national provisions (POPIA s.27(1), LGPD Art 11, Kenya DPA s.45(1)). Maturity `candidate`.
   - `consent-status`: nine values aligned with DPV v2's two-tier hierarchy: `requested`, `given`, `renewed`, `refused`, `withdrawn` (subject-initiated), `revoked` (controller-initiated), `expired`, `invalidated`, `unknown` (legacy imports where state cannot be determined). Maturity `candidate`.
   - `collection-medium`: four values from ISO 27560: `paper`, `electronic`, `verbal`, `mixed`.
   - `consent-expression`: values aligned with ISO 29184 Annex A: `opt-in-explicit`, `opt-in-signed`, `opt-in-witnessed`, `opt-in-biometric`, `opt-out`, `implied`. Orthogonal to `collection-medium` (a biometric opt-in is captured on an electronic medium; the two axes do not collapse).
   - `delegation-type`: six values: `self`, `legal-guardian`, `parent`, `power-of-attorney`, `authorised-representative`, `institutional-representative`. Each carries a DPV URI where DPV has an equivalent.
   - `withdrawal-channel`: eight values: `web`, `mobile`, `api`, `paper`, `verbal`, `in-person-office`, `community-worker`, `other`.
   - `recipient-role`: four values aligned with DPV and GDPR: `processor` (`dpv:DataProcessor`), `joint-controller` (`dpv:JointDataController`), `independent-controller` (`dpv:DataController`), `sub-processor`.

### B. `ConsentRecord` shape

6. **`ConsentRecord.data_subject` is ranged at `Party`.** The common case is a `Person`. `Group` consent (household, family) is operationally real and is how programs actually capture consent for heads of household. The concept's definition explicitly excludes `Farm` as a data subject (Farms are not natural persons or legal beneficiaries under any data protection law) and requires that `data_subject = Group` pair with `delegation_type ≠ self`. The alternative of narrowing to `Person` with N records per group was considered and rejected for operational fidelity; see Alternatives.
7. **`ConsentRecord.controllers` is ranged at `Organization` and is plural (`1..*`).** Single controllership is the common case; joint controllership (GDPR Art 26, Kenya DPA s.25, LGPD Art 26) is legally recognised and frequent in program delivery (ministry plus UN agency; government plus NGO implementing partner). An optional `joint_controller_arrangement_ref` (URI or identifier) points to the documented Art 26 arrangement.
8. **`ConsentRecord.recipients` is ranged at `Organization` and is plural (`0..*`).** Each recipient carries a `recipient_role` qualifier from the vocabulary in decision 5 (`processor`, `joint-controller`, `independent-controller`, `sub-processor`). The processor-versus-controller distinction is legally material and interop consumers need to read it off the record. Category-level recipient description (when specific organisations are not pre-identified) is carried by `allowed_recipient_categories`, ranged at a vocabulary of `organization-type`.
9. **`ConsentRecord.indicated_by` is ranged at `Agent`.** Matches ADR-008's pattern for `performed_by`, `evaluator`, and `publisher`. The common case is a `Person` (the data subject themselves when `delegation_type = self`, or a parent/caretaker/guardian for non-self delegation); the range admits `Organization` for public trustees and court-appointed corporate guardians where the law recognises them.
10. **Legal basis is required and dual-tier.** `ConsentRecord.legal_basis` is required, ranged at the `legal-basis` vocabulary. Where any URI in `personal_data_categories` resolves to a subclass of `dpv:SpecialCategoryPersonalData`, `ConsentRecord.special_category_basis` is additionally required, ranged at the `special-category-basis` vocabulary. A record with `legal_basis = consent` and biometric data but no `special_category_basis` is insufficient evidence of Art 9(2)(a) explicit consent; both grounds must be cited. `legal_basis_reference` (string) carries the local law citation (for example `"Kenya DPA 2019 s.30(1)(b)"`) to complement the GDPR-aligned vocabulary code. The SHACL shape generated from the pinned DPV snapshot (decision 20) performs the URI-resolution check for adopters who opt in; there is no parallel hand-curated boolean flag, which would silently drift against URI resolution.
11. **`ConsentRecord.consent_record_type` distinguishes internal records from receipts,** per ISO 27560: values are `internal_record` (controller's working state) and `receipt` (artefact delivered to the data subject). Both share the same concept and property set. `receipt` records are the target of the Consent Receipt VC pattern in decision 24. Named `consent_record_type` (not `record_type`) to avoid collision with the CRVS-scoped `record_type` property, which predates this ADR.
12. **Status lifecycle plus immutability annotation.** `ConsentRecord.status` takes values from `consent-status`. The schema does not enforce transition rules at the YAML level (consistent with how `Enrollment`, `PaymentEvent`, `Grievance` handle status). It marks terms-fields with an `immutable_after_status: given` annotation. The build pipeline emits this as an `rdfs:comment` plus a machine-readable `ps:immutableAfterStatus "given"` triple on each annotated property in the Turtle output. No `sh:PropertyShape` is emitted for immutability in v1: SHACL's standard validators lack access to the prior state needed to assert field-level immutability, and emitting a comment-only shape misrepresents SHACL semantics. Adopters enforce immutability in application code; a follow-on ADR may promote this to a real SHACL SPARQL constraint once the pyshacl dependency is acceptable. Terms-fields: `data_subject`, `controllers`, `recipients`, `recipient_role`, `allowed_recipient_categories`, `purposes`, `personal_data_categories`, `processing_operations`, `legal_basis`, `special_category_basis`, `notice_ref`, `notice_version`, `effective_date`, `jurisdiction`, `collection_medium`, `consent_expression`. Mutable-after-given fields: `status`, withdrawal fields, audit fields, `evidence_ref` (additive only: new attachments allowed, existing attachments not editable). Immutability is a legal property (GDPR Art 7(1) demonstrability), not a UX preference.
13. **`ConsentRecord` full property list** (reusing existing schema properties per ADR-007 where names and semantics match):
   - Identity and provenance: `identifier`, `external_id`, `consent_record_type`, `schema_version`, `conforms_to`, `created_at`, `collection_session_ref` (optional; superseded by `CollectionSession` concept in ADR-013).
   - Parties: `data_subject` (Party), `controllers` (Organization[]), `recipients` (Organization[]) with per-recipient `recipient_role`, `allowed_recipient_categories` (organization-type[]), `indicated_by` (Agent), `delegation_type`, `witnessed_by` (Person[]).
   - Processing: `purposes` (URI[], values are DPV URIs), `personal_data_categories` (URI[], values are DPV URIs), `processing_operations` (URI[], values are DPV URIs), `legal_basis`, `special_category_basis` (conditional), `legal_basis_reference` (string), `joint_controller_arrangement_ref` (string).
   - Notice: `notice_ref` (PrivacyNotice), `notice_version` (string pinned at agreement time), `notice_presentation_language` (ISO 639-3 code).
   - Validity and jurisdiction: `effective_date`, `expiry_date`, `signed_date` (the day the data subject indicated consent; may precede `effective_date` for paper workflows), `jurisdiction` (ISO 3166-1 alpha-2).
   - Collection evidence: `collection_medium`, `consent_expression`, `evidence_ref` (URI or identifier pointing to the evidence artefact), `verified_by` (Person, the data-entry verifier for paper flows), `verified_date`.
   - Status and withdrawal: `status`, `withdrawal_channel` (populated when status is `withdrawn`), `withdrawal_uri`, `withdrawal_reason`, `refusal_reason`.
   - Retention: `storage_duration_days` (integer, inline for v1; standalone `RetentionPolicy` deferred).

### C. `PrivacyNotice` shape

14. **`PrivacyNotice` full property list,** per ISO/IEC 29184 Section 5 and GDPR Art 13/14:
   - Identity: `identifier`, `name`, `version`, `supersedes_ref` (self-reference for version chains).
   - Validity: `effective_date`, `expiry_date` (optional), `jurisdiction` (ISO 3166-1 alpha-2).
   - Controller and DPO: `controller` (Organization), `dpo_contact` (structured contact or string).
   - Scope (the boundary within which consent records must fall): `purposes` (URI[], DPV URIs), `legal_basis` (legal-basis[]), `personal_data_categories` (URI[], DPV URIs), `processing_operations` (URI[], DPV URIs), `recipient_categories` (organization-type[]) or `recipients_described` (free-text when partners are not pre-identified).
   - Content: `retention_description`, `transfer_description` (nullable; international transfers), `automated_decision_description` (nullable; Art 22 profiling).
   - Rights and withdrawal: `rights_description`, `withdrawal_description`, `complaint_authority_contact`.
   - Languages: `available_languages` (ISO 639-3[]). One notice record covers all its localisations; localised text bodies use the existing translation mechanism.
   - Source (Art 14 indirect collection): `source_of_data` (nullable).

### D. Operational properties required by field practice

15. **Paper-to-digitisation gap is modelled explicitly.** `signed_date` is the day the data subject indicated consent, when known (may be absent for fully digital flows). `effective_date` is when the record becomes operative (typically equal to `signed_date`; may differ for post-dated or pre-dated agreements). `created_at` is the system digitisation timestamp, always populated. `evidence_ref` points to the scanned form. `verified_by` and `verified_date` capture the data-entry check. Non-optional in practice for paper-first programs; non-required in the schema because fully-digital adopters do not need them.
16. **Language of notice presentation is first-class.** `PrivacyNotice.available_languages` lists the languages in which the notice is published. `ConsentRecord.notice_presentation_language` records the language actually used when the notice was communicated to the data subject, which may be verbal translation into a language the notice was not published in (common in multilingual intake). Absence of this field is a documented gap that programs should populate where practical.
17. **Witnesses are modelled.** `ConsentRecord.witnessed_by` (Person[], `0..*`) records the identity of witnesses for verbal, thumbprint, or X-mark consent. When `collection_medium = verbal` or `consent_expression ∈ {opt-in-witnessed, opt-in-biometric}`, at least one witness should be recorded; adopters enforce this operationally.
18. **Jurisdiction is required on both concepts,** as ISO 3166-1 alpha-2, cardinality single. Applicable law determines retention, age thresholds, special-category grounds, transfer rules, and supervisory authority. A record or notice without a jurisdiction is operationally floating. Under joint controllership, this is the lead controller's primary establishment jurisdiction. Multi-jurisdictional records are the exceptional case; a follow-on ADR will lift cardinality if adopters hit the limit.
19. **Withdrawal channel and mechanism.** `ConsentRecord.withdrawal_channel` records how a withdrawal was actually received, populated when `status` transitions to `withdrawn`. `ConsentRecord.withdrawal_uri` is the published withdrawal mechanism (optional; may be empty for non-digital-only programs). Together these satisfy GDPR Art 7(3) documentability: the controller can show not only that withdrawal is possible but that it happened and through what channel.

### E. Interoperability hygiene

20. **DPV URI namespaces are pinned** in the build pipeline's JSON-LD context: `dpv: https://w3id.org/dpv#`, `dpv-pd: https://w3id.org/dpv/pd#`, `dpv-gdpr: https://w3id.org/dpv/legal/eu/gdpr#`, `dpv-tech: https://w3id.org/dpv/tech#`, `dpv-loc: https://w3id.org/dpv/loc#`. The current DPV version and a Turtle snapshot are stored under `external/dpv/` with a manifest recording version, source URL, fetched_at, sha256, and refresh procedure. If DPV publishes v3 with URI changes, a migration ADR is required; the schema does not silently track DPV releases. A graph-invariant test verifies that every DPV URI in `external_equivalents.dpv` and `external_equivalents.dpv-gdpr` resolves against the pinned snapshot (not a live fetch).
21. **`ConsentRecord.schema_version` is a dereferenceable URI,** for example `https://publicschema.org/ConsentRecord/v1`, that resolves to the generated JSON Schema, not an opaque version string. A companion `conforms_to` array lists external standard conformance claims: `urn:iso:std:iso-iec:ts:27560:2023`, `https://w3id.org/dpv#ConsentRecord`, and Kantara Consent Receipt v1.1 URI when `record_type = receipt`. `PrivacyNotice.schema_version` follows the same pattern.
22. **System mappings on day one cover:** OpenSPP `spp.consent.*` models (direct field mapping), W3C DPV v2 URIs (structural mirroring per decision 4), ISO/IEC TS 27560:2023 (for record_type and structural fidelity), Kantara Consent Receipt v1.1 (receipt type only, with a property-by-property crosswalk document because field names diverge from DPV), FHIR R4 `Consent` and FHIR R5 `Consent` (documented as imperfect with per-version crosswalks; the R4 and R5 `provision` hierarchies do not map 1:1 to DPV; R5's `verification` backbone and `sourceAttachment` validate the paper-workflow fields in decision 15), GovStack Consent Building Block (API-first OpenAPI specification for digital government consent; mapped as a subset because GovStack is consent-only, single-controller, and digital-only and does not cover non-consent legal bases, paper intake, or VC export). DCI Core has no consent concept and is documented as a gap.

### F. Non-decisions documented here

23. **Lifecycle rules live in `docs/consent-lifecycle.md`, not in the YAML,** with the exception of the `immutable_after_status` annotation (decision 12). Documented rules include: valid status transitions, notice-as-boundary subset containment, re-consent triggers (substantive versus editorial notice updates), age-of-majority transitions (adopters handle via `expiry_date` on parental consents), consent-versus-eligibility separation, performance guidance (summary caches, indexing on `(status, expiry_date)`), the operational cost of re-consent campaigns (multi-month field operations, not database transactions), and a decision tree for "when should a program actually claim `legal_basis = consent`?" (most programs over-claim; public interest and legal obligation are frequently the honest choice). OpenSPP's `spp_consent` module is cited as the reference implementation for runtime enforcement.
24. **The Consent Receipt VC pattern lives in `docs/selective-disclosure.md`** as a worked example of SD-JWT VC for a `ConsentRecord` with `record_type = receipt`. Must-disclose claims: `purposes`, `legal_basis`, `controllers`, `effective_date`, `expiry_date`, `withdrawal_uri`. Legitimately selective: specific `recipients` (category may be disclosed while identities stay hidden), data-subject identifier, individual `personal_data_categories`. Consistent with Kantara Consent Receipt v1.1 intent and ISO 27560's receipt semantics. Aligned with the W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model 2.0.
25. **No backward-compatibility shims.** No adopters are on the v2 schema. Breaking changes are free now; they become expensive on day one of v1.0.

## Alternatives considered

- **DPV alignment levels: map via `system_mappings` only versus mirror structurally versus publish a formal DPV profile.** The first draft chose "map only". Expert review pushed toward structural mirroring. Chosen: mirror. Rejected: map only, because it forces adopters to bridge two concept trees for every interop hop and produces divergence incentives. Rejected: full DPV profile, because publishing a DPVCG-compatible profile is a political process (submission, review, listing) disproportionate to v1 scope and commits the schema to DPV's release cadence. Mirroring preserves editorial control while giving adopters DPV compliance by default.
- **Rename `ConsentRecord` to `ProcessingRecord` or `LawfulProcessingRecord`.** The privacy-law review argues the current name misrepresents non-consent records. Rejected for v1: DPV and ISO 27560 use `ConsentRecord` as the published name for the superset, and diverging from the standards' primary name (which is how adopters will search and map) costs more than the naming mismatch saves. The concept definition addresses the concern with ruthless first-sentence honesty: records with `legal_basis ≠ consent` are still named `ConsentRecord` by the standards' convention, and the schema's own lifecycle guidance in `docs/consent-lifecycle.md` instructs programs away from over-claiming consent. Revisit if a regulator publishes a finding on the naming.
- **Narrow `data_subject` to `Person`** with `Group` consent modelled as N records sharing an `indicated_by`. Rejected for v1: operational fidelity. Heads of household sign consent at community gatherings; practitioners and MIS platforms model this as one record with a `Group` subject plus a delegation. Splitting into N records is defensible on legal-purity grounds but fights how intake actually happens. The Farm exclusion in the definition (decision 6) handles the only case where the broader Party range is semantically wrong.
- **Single-cardinality `controller`.** Rejected: joint controllership (GDPR Art 26) is the norm in program delivery.
- **`indicated_by` narrowed to `Person`.** Rejected: public trustees and court-appointed corporate guardians are legally recognised in several jurisdictions. Ranged at `Agent` following ADR-008's pattern for `performed_by`.
- **Model `ConsentRecord` as an `Event` subtype.** Rejected: the record is a standing agreement with a validity period, not a point-in-time happening. The closer structural precedent is `ScoringRule` (stable artefact referenced by events), not `Enrollment`.
- **Split into `ConsentAgreement` (standing) and `ConsentEvent` (lifecycle).** A candidate structural analogue to `ScoringRule + ScoringEvent`. Rejected for v1 on interoperability grounds: DPV, ISO 27560, Kantara, FHIR Consent, and OpenSPP all collapse agreement and event into one record with a status field. Splitting would give a cleaner RDF graph but force every serialiser to stitch the two back together to match the published standards. Revisit if adopters find the status-field shape limiting for audit or replay semantics.
- **Define `ProcessingPurpose`, `PersonalDataCategory`, and `ProcessingOperation` as PublicSchema concepts mirroring DPV class trees.** Considered in an earlier draft on the grounds that PublicSchema's concept-factoring rule (hierarchy or per-value properties means concept) appears to apply. Rejected: values on these axes are referenced on records, not themselves records with lifecycles, properties, or adopter-specific state. Forking DPV's class hierarchies into PublicSchema concepts would duplicate W3C work, require ongoing sync against DPV releases, and offer no operational value that direct URI reference does not already deliver. Curated guidance lives in `docs/dpv-vocabulary-reference.md` rather than in the schema. Reversible: if adopters later need per-value properties beyond what DPV itself carries (for example, a social-protection-specific sensitivity flag), the axis in question can be promoted to a concept without changing how existing records serialise, because the property ranges remain URI-compatible.
- **Keep `consent-purpose`, `personal-data-category`, `processing-operation` as flat closed PublicSchema vocabularies.** Rejected for the same reason as above: DPV already publishes these as hierarchies with URIs. A flat closed vocabulary would either be lossier than DPV (and force adopters to bridge) or would duplicate the DPV hierarchy without adding value. Direct DPV URI reference is simpler and preserves hierarchy queries where adopters need them.
- **Single-axis `collection-method`** conflating medium (paper, electronic) and expression (signed, witnessed, biometric). Rejected: the axes are orthogonal. A biometric opt-in is still captured on an electronic medium; collapsing the two loses information that regulators and auditors need.
- **Full DPV sync via `build/sync_standards.py`.** Rejected for v1, but not on size grounds (DPV is small). Rejected on editorial grounds: full DPV includes purposes (marketing, advertising, commercial profiling) irrelevant to public program delivery. A curated subset plus structural mirroring is the correct stance. A future sync that maps curated values to a pinned DPV release is feasible; hand curation against pinned v2 is the v1 choice.
- **Split into three ADRs (009a concepts, 009b Consent Receipt VC, 009c governance companions).** Considered; the schema reviewer argued for this. Rejected: the concepts and the VC pattern are tightly coupled (decision 11's `record_type` directly enables decision 24's VC pattern), and splitting would spread one coherent decision across two reviews without lowering total cost. Governance companions (DSAR, DataSharingAgreement, RetentionPolicy, CollectionSession, ROPA, DataBreachRecord) are genuinely separable and are named as follow-on ADRs in Follow-on work.
- **Encode notice-as-boundary subset check in SHACL.** Deferred: expressible (SHACL supports subset constraints via `sh:in` and property paths) but adds validator weight and duplicates what adopters enforce in application code. The `immutable_after_status` annotation (decision 12) is the one exception where schema-level encoding earns its keep. Revisit if adopters ask for validator-level subset enforcement.
- **Inline `PrivacyNotice` on `ConsentRecord`.** Rejected: ISO 29184 treats the notice as a separate, versioned artefact precisely because one notice governs many consent records. Inlining duplicates the legal text per record and loses the versioned-artefact property that adopters need for "which notice was shown to whom, when" queries.
- **`recipient_mode` (specific versus category) as a schema concept.** Rejected: implementation optimisation. PublicSchema exposes both `recipients` (specific organisations) and `allowed_recipient_categories` (typed categories). Adopters choose either or both per record; the schema does not require a mode selector.
- **Separate audit-trail concept.** Rejected: runtime concern. Adopters who need an immutable ledger attach one through implementation (OpenSPP's `spp.consent.history`).
- **Place consent under a `privacy/` or `governance/` domain segment.** Rejected for now: consent is universal. A new domain code for a handful of concepts is premature. Revisit if we accumulate enough governance concepts to justify the segment; a future ADR can carve it out without breaking flat URIs via aliases.

## Consequences

The schema gains a data-governance layer. Root namespace adds two concepts (`ConsentRecord`, `PrivacyNotice`) and ten vocabularies (`consent-record-type`, `legal-basis`, `special-category-basis`, `consent-status`, `collection-medium`, `consent-expression`, `delegation-type`, `withdrawal-channel`, `recipient-role`, `organization-type`). Purposes, personal data categories, and processing operations are carried as DPV URIs on properties, not as PublicSchema concepts or vocabularies. The increment is smaller than an earlier draft proposed, and the reduced surface area reduces drift risk against DPV. Build pipeline and site rendering absorb through existing patterns. The site navigation gains a "data governance" grouping that collects these concepts for discovery without changing their URIs.

Mirroring DPV gives adopters DPV compliance by default and reduces reinvention. It binds the schema to DPV's versioning cadence: a DPV v3 with breaking URI changes triggers a migration ADR, not silent drift. A pinned snapshot plus a graph-invariant test catches drift at build time.

`Party`, `Organization`, `Agent`, and `Person` gain new consumers without modification. ADR-008's Agent supertype becomes load-bearing for `indicated_by`; ADR-007's property-reuse pattern applies to `effective_date`, `expiry_date`, `jurisdiction`. The root namespace stays flat.

Joint controllership, the processor-versus-controller distinction, and dual-tier legal basis (Art 6 plus Art 9) are all representable. The schema now serves regulated multi-organisation arrangements common in program delivery without adopter workarounds.

Practitioner additions (paper workflow, language, witnesses, withdrawal channel) make the schema fit field intake. Fully digital adopters carry the fields as optional without cost. Paper-first adopters can represent their workflow without local extensions.

The `immutable_after_status` annotation makes consent-term immutability machine-readable without requiring runtime enforcement at the schema level. Adopters can opt into the generated SHACL shape. Non-enforcement remains possible but is framed in `docs/consent-lifecycle.md` as a compliance choice with legal consequences, not a UX preference.

Value-level versus vocabulary-level maturity: `legal-basis` and `special-category-basis` values are locked by statute; the vocabulary itself is marked `candidate`. Some vocabularies are `draft` at the vocabulary level because the curated value set may expand, even when individual values are standards-pinned. `docs/versioning-and-maturity.md` acquires a clarifying paragraph.

VC Data Model 2.0 alignment gets its first concrete case study. The Consent Receipt VC pattern (decision 24) clarifies must-disclose versus selectively-disclosable claims for a consent record, which surfaces disclosure-profile design choices that later VC-shaped concepts can reuse.

The `/sp/` and `/crvs/` domain concepts are unchanged. No existing URIs move.

Deferred scope remains large and legally material: DSAR (the gap between withdrawal and erasure), DataSharingAgreement (controller-to-controller and processor contracts), RetentionPolicy (currently inline on `ConsentRecord.storage_duration_days`), CollectionSession (the practitioner's bulk-intake grouping), RecordOfProcessingActivities (GDPR Art 30), DataBreachRecord, international transfer properties (`transfer_basis`), and `capacity_basis` for age-of-majority and legal-capacity transitions. Each is named in Follow-on work. Until the DSAR concept lands, the "withdrawal ≠ erasure" distinction is a documented gap in `docs/consent-lifecycle.md`, not a schema feature.

Maturity: both concepts `draft`; most vocabularies `draft`; `legal-basis`, `special-category-basis`, and `consent-status` `candidate`. Promotion to `candidate` for the concepts gates on two or three adopter integration attempts plus review by a privacy-law expert and a DPVCG contributor.
